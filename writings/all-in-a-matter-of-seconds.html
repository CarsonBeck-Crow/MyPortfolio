All in a Matter of Seconds

By Carson Beck

If you’re reading this, I very well may be dead. My name was Jason Loyd. Today was my 23rd birthday. Growing up, I’d always loved superhero comics. The Flash had always been my favorite; super speed always seemed like such a limitless power. In movies and TV shows, they’d depict speedsters moving through time at a normal pace while everything around them slowed to a crawl. It almost seemed like super speed and the ability to slow down time were one and the same. Which, realistically, maybe there is no difference at the end of the day. I suppose it depends on the perspective. Anyway, all this to explain my current situation. By the time you’re reading this, I’ll have been gone for possibly a few minutes, maybe half an hour, or even a few hours. For me, it’s been years.

You don’t know me, of course. I’m not from here. I walked to this city a few months ago looking for some books on physics and theories about relativity. I found a few things, but nothing tangible.

See, I had been having a pretty terrible birthday. I’d lost my job, I’d been splashed with gutter water by a passing car, and my girlfriend was going to be in another city for the next two weeks. When I got home, though, things took a turn, and everything started to get a lot better. She’d come home to surprise me. She was sitting in our tiny little apartment, a few of our friends had come to celebrate as well, and she’d handed me a cupcake with a single lit candle. I made a wish, one that I now regret for the rest of my life.

Time suddenly seemed to freeze around me. I was so confused, so scared, and I started to panic. I left my apartment, only to find that the world around me had come to a standstill. I found out very quickly that I couldn’t use my phone. It seemed basically unresponsive to my touches and gestures. I tried to speak with people, I tried to use computers, but nothing worked. After what felt like half a day had passed, I pulled my phone out again to find that the screen had shattered. Everything started to fit together. I found myself at my local library, and I sat in front of a clock watching the hands and waiting. I waited for what, to me, felt like hours, but I couldn’t leave. I had to confirm what was going on. Finally, I saw the second hand of the clock begin to move at a snail's pace before it came to a halt again.

I didn’t know at the time just how fast I was moving, but I’ve since learned. For me, each second of real time is equivalent to a full 24 hours. Which means, at the normal pace I’m walking, I’m actually moving at 99.9999999815% of the speed of light, or 671,080,885.53 mph. It took me forever to figure that out. I spent what felt like a full month agonizing over that single thing. Not being able to use calculators really destroyed me. The thing about moving at that speed is that I should be dead. My body should have burned to ash, and I should have been incinerated. At least, that’s what physics would say. However, I seem to have developed a number of other adaptive traits to let me survive these speeds. My body’s durability is insane. Believe me, once I realized what was happening, I tried to end it all. I jumped off a ten-story building and crashed down into the asphalt street below. I walked away with barely a scratch. I could see a few cracks forming on the street when I landed, and when I went back there like a day or two later, there was a massive crater in the ground. My body seems to be shielded from wind friction, which is certainly a blessing—not just for me, but for every person I came across. They would likely be vaporized by the heat of an object moving at these speeds.

Frankly, I don’t even know if these words will ever reach anyone. I’ve been writing everything down, but for all I know, this whole journal is going to burst into flames in a few days, given how fast I’m writing in it. I guess we’ll just have to see. There are other things too. I don’t breathe anymore. I don’t sleep. I don’t eat or drink. In comics, speedsters have to consume more food than anyone else because their bodies are working at such high rates. For me, though, it seems like my body adapted by turning those functions off. Theoretically, I’m immortal now, I think, but I’m not entirely sure. It’s possible that my body may still age. Not enough time has passed yet for me to be able to tell. Truthfully, I hope that I will age and die. I already feel as if I’m losing myself in this place. I haven’t spoken to anyone in a year. I rarely hear sounds at all. Light is roughly a million times faster than sound, so it never really gets to me in time.

I’m so scared. I’m so alone. I just want this to be over. I want this to stop. I want to be back home. I want to be with my family and friends. I’ve been back to see them a number of times, and it always breaks my heart. I see the look on my girlfriend’s face, frozen in horror, as she watches me disappear instantaneously. For her, I was there one second and gone the next. The only way she or anyone else could perceive me, even for 0.1 seconds, would require me to stand completely still for 144 days in my time. Even in my state of not needing to sleep, eat, or breathe, I don’t know if I could ever achieve that. I’m stuck in an eternal hell of being completely invisible and isolated from a world that barely moves around me. And there’s absolutely nothing I can do about it. At first, the idea was almost cosmic horror-esque—something you’d see on The Twilight Zone or read about in a Lovecraft story—but after so long of existing here, the dread has begun to change. I’m beginning to slip into an uncomfortable feeling of lethargy.

Back in the beginning, there was a time when I thought there was someone else here with me. Or something else, maybe. Every now and then, I’d see something moving just outside my vision. Or I’d blink, and something would flicker across my eyes, like when drivers start to fall asleep at the wheel and their brains trick them into seeing something run out in front of them to shock them awake. It didn’t take me long to understand what it was. I was losing my mind. I was hallucinating. Things got really bad for a time. The hallucinations plagued me frequently, and I could never quite glimpse them. It was breaking my psyche. That was when I learned that I could no longer enter water. Due to the speed I’m moving, the time it takes for the surface tension to break is far too long. It’s probably for the best, given all the other quirks of my curse, I’d probably end up stuck at the bottom of the ocean or something. That was before I’d learned that I was no longer breathing anyway. I wanted to drown myself. Sometime after that, the hallucinations largely faded. I think the shock of being pushed to the point of trying to drown myself, and being hit with the realization that I couldn’t, had broken something inside me. I still see those hallucinations sometimes. They creep in every once in a while.

I’ve been reading a lot. It’s one of the few things I’m able to do at these speeds. Like I said before, I’m learning as much as I can about physics and theories like the theory of relativity. However, so much of it has gone over my head, and I’m starting to lose hope that books like those would even help me anyway. I mean, surely if there was anything that could help me in one of those books, we’d have figured out how to travel at light speed already, right? I don’t know. I have no idea what to do. I don’t even really know what caused this. I made a silly fucking wish on a birthday cupcake, for fuck’s sake. Now I’m stuck in this fucked existence. I have no idea if anyone will ever know what happened to me. I have no idea what to do. I thought about leaving this with my girlfriend or my family, but I couldn’t bring myself to do it. The thought of them reading this killed me inside. What little left there is to kill. That’s why I brought it to you. Not that it’ll do me any good. I mean, by the time you’re done reading this, who knows how long will have passed. For all I know, all the moving around I’ve been doing is speeding up global warming or something, and I’m gonna bring about the end of the fucking world. Not that I’ll be coherent enough to see it.

I have a plan. It’s not a way out. It’s not freedom from this curse. It’s just the end. I won’t be able to get out of this existence. I just won’t. I came to accept that over the course of writing this. The little hope I had left has gone from me, and all I have now are these words on these pages. I’m going to walk across the surface of the planet and see all the beautiful places I once wanted to visit, and when that’s all said and done, I’m going to find the nearest ocean, sit on top of the water, and wait. I’ll wait, and wait, and wait until I sink to the bottom of the ocean. Perhaps I’ll explore the undersea if I’m able to, but most likely, I’ll just sit there, beneath the water, waiting for my brain to shut off due to the agony of time, or due to death by aging. Whichever comes first. If neither comes, then I’ll just have to think of something else.

Thank you for listening to my story. I appreciated your company while I sat and wrote this. I was sitting just next to you the whole time. Of course, by the time you’re seeing this, I’ll be long gone. Across the planet, or sleeping beneath the waves. My name was Jason Loyd. This was the story of how I became cursed with super speed.
